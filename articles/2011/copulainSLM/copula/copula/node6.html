Copulas in Sinhala and Tamil

Colloquial Sinhala has no copula as such, but uses a predicative suffix -yi on adjectives ending with a vowel ex:sinh:yi:adj:v. Adjectives which end in a consonant ex:sinh:yi:adj:c, and other word classes ending in any sound do not take this suffix ex:sinh:yi:n.

mee amu miris hari særa-yi COLLOQUIAL SINHALA
prox raw chillis really strong-YI
`These green chillies are really strong.' (Gair, 2003: 794)

mee dawaswl haal bohom ganaŋ(*-yi) SINHALA
prox days rice very expensive(-YI)
`These days (uncooked) rice is very expensive.' (Gair, 2003: 795)

mam govi-yek(*-yi) SINHALA
1S farmer-INDEF(-YI)
`I am a farmer.' (Gair, 1998: 241)

In Literary Sinhala, a similar suffix is also used for nominal predications, as in ex:sinh:yi:classical.

mama goviyekmi LITERARY SINHALA
1S-NOM farmer.NOM.1SG
`I am a farmer.' (Gair, 1998: 242)

There is some discussion of whether mi in should indeed be analyzed as a copula, or rather as an agreement suffix (Gair, 1998: 242). Whatever the right analysis of -mi and similar forms, it is clear that the variety of Sinhala the Malays were exposed to did not feature this form. The most copula-like element they are likely to have encountered is -yi, which does not suggest itself as possible model for the Sri Lanka Malay copula. Its form as a suffix is very different from the verbal form employed by Sri Lanka Malay, and its use for adjectival predications is precisely one where the use of the Sri Lanka Malay copula is not found.

In Tamil, the existential iru is sometimes analyzed as a copula. This verb is used for predications of existence as in ex:tamil:irukkiratu:exist but also for locational predications as in ex:tamil:irukkiratu:loc.

kumaar-ukku oru paiyan iru-kkir-aan TAMIL
Kumar-DAT one boy IRU-PRES-3SM
`Kumar has one boy/There exists one boy to Kumar.' (Lehmann, 1989: 188)

inta7#7k2 kiraama-tt-il muunru koovil (iru-kkir-atu) TAMIL
this village-OBL-LOC three temple IRU-PRES-3SN
`There are three temples in this village.' (Lehmann, 1989: 173)

Uses for property word predications ex:tamil:irukkiratu:adj and nominal predications ex:tamil:irukkiratu:n are ungrammatical.

paippu mukkiyam (*irukkiratu) TAMIL
education importance (IRU.3SN)
`Education is important.' (Lehmann, 1989: 172)

kumaar vakkiil (*irukkiratu/*irukkiraan) TAMIL
Kumar lawyer (IRU.3SN/IRU.3SM)
`Kumar is a lawyer.' (Lehmann, 1989: 171)

While in ex:tamil:irukkiratu:adj and ex:tamil:irukkiratu:n the English translational equivalent has a copula, it does not seem pertinent to grant the same status to the Tamil form. An analysis as an existential can cover both the meanings given in ex:tamil:irukkiratu:exist and ex:tamil:irukkiratu:loc, namely `A boy exists (for Kumar)' and `Temple exist in this village', while excluding the readings `Education exists importance' and `Kumar exists a lawyer'. This is precisely what we find in ex:tamil:irukkiratu:adj and ex:tamil:irukkiratu:n. It is true that iru is sometimes found in nominal predications as in ex:tamil:irukkiratu:adv, but in that case, the noun is adverbialized by aakalaay, and the construction can actually be shown to have a locational reading as well (cf. Lehmann, 1989: 174).

kumaar vakkiil-aakalaay iru-nt-aan TAMIL
Kumar lawyer-ADV IRU-PAST-3SM
`Kumar was a lawyer.' (Lehmann, 1989: 174)

Whatever analysis one prefers for iru, this Tamil form seems to be closer related to the Sri Lanka Malay form aada, which would be used for translations of ex:tamil:irukkiratu:exist and ex:tamil:irukkiratu:loc, given in ex:tamil:irukkiratu:slm:loc and ex:tamil:irukkiratu:slm:exist.

Ini nigiri=ka thiiga koovil aada SLM
PROX village=LOC three temple exist
`There are three (Hindu) temples in this village.'

Kumaar=nang hatthu aanak klaaki aada SLM
Kumar=DAT one child male exist
`Kumar has one boy.'

The parallels between iru and (asÃ )dhaathang(apa) are less striking, and it seems unlikely that Sri Lanka Malay emulated the Tamil form iru with (asÃ )dhaathang(apa).

Sinhala and Tamil both have conjunctive participles, too. The conjunctive particple forms of the verb meaning `to come' in Sinhala (ævillaa) and Tamil (vantu) are not used in a copular sense in these languages; they can only be used in the original reading of temporal anteriority.

lamayaa pantiya=a ævillaa,
child class=DAT come.CP
`The child came to the class and/The child having come to class,' liyuma livvaa SINHALA
letter write
`(he) wrote the letter.' (Karunatillake, 2004: 161)

naaai kumaar va-ntu,
tomorrow Kumar come-CP
`Tomorrow Kumar will come and/Kumar having come tomorrow,' enka-ai7#7c canti-pp-aan TAMIL
1PL-ACC meet-FUT-3SM
`(he) will meet us.' (Lehmann, 1989: 267)

To sum up, while the Malay ancestor language, Sinhala, and Tamil all have structures which come at least close to a copula, none of these structures is similar enough to the SLM structure as to suggest itself as the driving force behind the development of the copula in Sri Lanka Malay. It thus seems likely that the copula in Sri Lanka Malay is an independent development.
